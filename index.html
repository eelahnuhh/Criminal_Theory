THEORY

What is Social Structure Theory?

Social Structure Theory is ‚Äúthe view that disadvantaged economic class position is a primary cause of crime‚Äù (Siegel, 2019, p. 175). Rather than psychological or biological problems an individual may have, social structure theory suggests that a person's place in society influences their chances of becoming a criminal. For crime to show a steady decrease, this theory urges that the societal structure be addressed and managed. Poor people are some of the most likely to commit crimes because of a system that keeps them at the bottom. They believe they are unable to achieve social success and boost their social status, so they commit crimes to move themselves and/or their families into a better standard of living.

EXPLANATIONS

Three Branches of Social Structure Theory

Social Strain Theory

Social strain theory is one branch of social structure that says that crime is a direct result of frustration and anger from individuals in a low socioeconomic class. People want to grow monetarily and become wealthier, but oftentimes there are not enough resources to allow this to occur.

"Strain Theory argues that crime occurs when there aren't enough legitimate opportunities for people to achieve the normal success goals of a society. In such a situation there is a 'strain' between the goals and the means to achieve those goals, and some people turn to crime in order to achieve success" (Thompson, 2023).

Failure to achieve positively valued goals and a disjunction of expectations and achievements leaves people helpless and confused on why they cannot break from the hardships they are in. Each type of strain increases the chance that negative emotions will occur within an individual and causes crime to be a more likely factor in order to break out of this unfortunate cycle.

Social Disorganization Theory

Social disorganization theory is another branch of social structure theory that says that people must be aligned with each other in order to keep things orderly in a community. Areas with high population turnover rates, diverse populations with many different ideas on how the community should be run, and poverty are more likely to experience high levels of crime due to a greater chance of weakened social bonds.

"Social disorganization is defined as an inability of community members to achieve shared values or to solve jointly experienced problems" (Community Correlates, 2003). Communities thrive when the citizens are able to share their values with each other and build one another up. Conflicting viewpoints and different ideas about life, religion, schools, etc cause a divide within a community which leads to a weak area that is more susceptible to uproars and breakouts of criminal behavior. An area that people do not enjoy living in, that has limited opportunities for growth, can easily turn into something worse when the people of the community do not care to keep it thriving.

Cultural Deviance Theory

Combines effects of social disorganization and strain to explain how people who are living in lower class neighborhoods react to social isolation and economic issues. The distance from people in your community and a lack of resources leads to strain from most people wanting to get better and do better within their community, but they just cannot.

Trapped in a low socioeconomic area where lower-class cultural norms have become so popular causes the likelihood of young people in the community to move towards violent or illegal behavior. The more popular crime becomes within an area, the more likely it will be that more and more people will turn to a life of violence and crime because it is all that they see and know.

CRIME

Personal Thoughts On Crime

Crime is an issue that plagues the lives of many all around the world. Many different factors contribute to why someone can and will commit a crime. Before taking this class, I believed the causes of crime were from someone just wanting to commit a crime just for the sake of it, greed, or the inability to provide for yourself and others around you.

After taking this course I now realize how much more goes into someone becoming a criminal. The areas you grow up in, the people around you, and opportunity all play a role in what you will become in the future. Social interaction directly affects how likely someone is to start or want to start committing crimes simply because of familiarity and learning.

It is imperative that people work together to create a strong community where everyone can thrive. Crime is not something that should only fall onto the individual though. The way society is shaped is made for certain people to stay at the bottom, and for others to rise up and live comfortable lives. Looking towards the government when issues come about, and working hard as a community, can be a good way to start meaningful change.

Labeling Theory is another part of social structure theory that explains why crime may continue even after punishment, or before someone has even committed a crime. Labeling theory may target people who have not broken the law, but because they look a certain way or live in a certain area they may be approached as if they had committed a crime more often (Barkan, 2016). This causes someone to believe that they must be destined to be a criminal because of how often they are being looked at as one.

On the other hand, an ex-convict has to live the rest of their lives (typically) holding on to the crime they committed even after serving time. This is shown in cases where you must check a box on a job application on whether you have served time before. Having to mention or explain past instances where someone broke the law can cause the individual to have a negative self-image and possibly succumb to the temptation of committing another crime in order to be around people who are more closely related to them.

Removing the pressure of past actions once someone has served their time, in my opinion, should be standard. Individuals should be able to come back into society and have opportunities to get good jobs and live a normal life. Too often people will come out of prison understanding the mistakes they made in the past and ready to come back to their families and lives. However, the constant reminder of mistakes from the past and labeling when trying to get better opportunities leads someone to feel defeated and often turn back to crime.
